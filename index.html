Strategic brief
Client objectives, audience profile, and engagement scope

The New Criterion is among the most critically regarded arts and literary publications in the United States. The strategic brief was unambiguous: build a video identity from near-zero capable of driving TNC+ subscription conversion, while establishing a repeatable content architecture sustainable across a monthly publishing cadence.

The audience profile presented a specific creative constraint: this readership is not persuaded by conventional content marketing tactics. The creative challenge was not to make the brand accessible — it was to make the video output worthy of the brand.

Target audienceSophisticated readers — arts, literary criticism, cultural commentary
Pre-engagement statusMinimal video presence, no established visual identity
Primary objectiveTNC+ subscription acquisition; audience retention via monthly content
Content cadenceMonthly issue content + annual tentpole + gala event production
Audience intelligence + creative insight
The understanding that governed every production decision

The New Criterion's readership evaluates content rather than merely consuming it. Algorithmic pacing or promotional framing would actively undermine subscriber trust. The strategic insight: this audience already carries strong brand affinity. The video content's primary function was not persuasion — it was confirmation.

"The audience doesn't need to be converted. They need the production to be worthy of their attention — which is a much higher bar."
A specific editorial decision — in full
The one choice that defined the visual identity

The most consequential single decision was the choice of shot duration in the tentpole subscription campaign. The first cut ran at an average shot length of approximately 3.2 seconds — conventional for editorial content of this type. A test screening suggested the pacing was actively undermining the piece: the cuts were arriving before the viewer had finished forming an impression of what they were looking at.

The revision extended average shot duration to 6.1 seconds — nearly double. Several shots ran for 9–11 seconds. What had read as competent production began reading as deliberate authority. The lesson was not that slow is always better. It was that for this audience, patience was itself a form of respect — and that the edit's primary job was to honour the pace at which an intelligent person processes a beautiful image.

The transferable principle
"Editorial pacing is not a production decision — it is an audience decision. The correct shot duration is the one that matches the cognitive rhythm of the specific viewer you're trying to reach."

Project overview
Scope, subject matter, and editorial mandate

Wolverines is a feature-length documentary following the Westview Wolverines — a high school football program in Omaha, Nebraska — across a complete competitive season. The film operates simultaneously as a season narrative and as a cultural portrait of the structural role athletics play in midwestern community identity.

Razak served as sole editor from assembly through picture lock, working from raw field production without a supervising editor or institutional framework. The editorial mandate was to construct a throughline that honoured the temporal stakes of the season arc while sustaining a macro-level cultural argument that extended well beyond any single game.

Editorial methodology + narrative architecture
Structural approach and audio-picture synchronisation

The primary editorial problem was emotional sync — identifying gameplay and vérité footage that carried the same emotional register as the corresponding interview content, so that image and audio function as a unified statement rather than an illustrated narration.

The working methodology was audio-led from assembly through picture lock: the energy, dynamic range, and tonal register of the audio track governed picture selection and shot duration at every stage — producing edits that feel organically paced rather than rhythmically imposed.

"In documentary, the edit is not an arrangement of images. It is an argument — and the audio is the thesis."
01
Audio-driven shot selection. The dynamic energy and tonal register of the audio track governs the kinetic load a given image can sustain. Misalignment produces subconscious viewer dissonance — this ratio is actively managed at every cut point.
02
Controlled energy escalation. Energy is introduced incrementally and withdrawn before it becomes perceptible as craft. The threshold between emotionally effective and editorially self-conscious is narrow — disciplined restraint is the primary tool.
03
Motivated camera movement. Camera moves are selected in reference to the directional and kinetic language of adjacent shots — creating continuity of motion that renders cut points perceptually invisible.
04
Grain and artifact matching. Digital origination is treated with matching grain structure and analog artifacts to achieve tonal continuity across mixed-source timelines.
05
Depth of field as editorial direction. Focus plane and depth of field are used as primary tools for viewer attention management — guiding the eye within the frame without requiring additional cutting.
Craft reflection — what I got wrong first
An honest account of a significant editorial revision

The assembly cut front-loaded the season arc — four of the first six sequences were game footage, with character and community material arriving late. The logic was intuitive: establish stakes early, deepen later. In practice it produced a film that felt like sports coverage with cultural commentary attached, rather than a cultural document that happened to use a football season as its spine.

The revision reversed the structural priority. The first act became almost entirely character and community — the football was withheld until the viewer had been given a reason to care about the people playing it. It required cutting approximately 11 minutes from the first third and rebuilding the game footage hierarchy entirely. The result was a film that sustained its argument from the opening frame rather than arriving at it.

The lesson
"The instinct to establish stakes immediately through action is almost always wrong in documentary. Restraint in the first act is the most important structural decision an editor makes — it determines whether the audience is watching a story or enduring one."

What this project demonstrates about the editorial practice

Wolverines represents the most complete expression of Razak's editorial voice — because the visual language was not inherited from a pre-existing brand framework, it was developed in response to the material itself.

Creative audit — before + after
Diagnosing the existing creative and reframing the visual platform

The existing Nordstrom Rack advertising operates in the visual grammar of off-price retail — high energy, price-forward, promotional. The product being advertised is not off-price in character: it is designer merchandise at off-price. The creative audit reveals a category-wide failure to distinguish between these two things, and an opportunity to build a brand identity that exploits that gap directly.

Existing creative
High energy, promotional framing. Price-forward. Indistinguishable from category competitors. Hook rate hypothesis: average or below — no pattern interrupt.
Luxury benchmark
Loro Piana, Tom Ford, Celine register. Long shot duration. Texture-led. No price communication. This is what the product deserves — and what the audience responds to.
The strategic insight
Why the contrast is the campaign

The price reveal mechanism works because it exploits a cognitive gap: if the viewer spends 22 seconds forming the impression that this is a $3,000 brand film, the price reveal creates a genuine moment of surprise. That surprise is the emotional hook. The product has not changed — only the visual frame around it. The creative strategy is to make the visual frame do the selling, and let the price close it.

"The hook psychology: pattern interrupt — it doesn't look like a Nordstrom Rack ad. Curiosity gap — what brand is this? Payoff — the price reveal closes the loop."
The edit — production brief
Execution framework for the 30-second spec

Structure: Open on texture — not product. No cut before 2 seconds. Grade: warm shadows, pulled highlights, 15–20% grain. Music: sparse piano or ambient strings, no lyrics. Hold the final wide shot one beat longer than feels natural. Price super at 22s: white text, no animation, 2 full seconds alone. Logo at 26s. Out.

Reference films: Loro Piana Fall 2024 · Tom Ford Winter 2024 Shanghai · Gucci × Gosling Road Trip · Celine / Hedi Slimane. Note average shot duration in each. Match it.

Performance rationale
Why this creative approach outperforms the category

Luxury-register visual grammar drives stronger upper-funnel performance in off-price retail because it creates a genuine pattern interrupt: the viewer registers a mismatch between the visual tone and the category expectation. That cognitive gap is attention. The price reveal converts that attention into surprise — one of the most effective triggers for both sharing behaviour and purchase intent.

The 3-second view rate is the first filter any paid social creative must clear. Luxury-register content passes at a structurally higher rate because it does not immediately read as an advertisement. The CTR premium comes from the reveal — which rewards viewers who stayed through the hold.

Strategic diagnosis
Why conventional reputation management fails here

The instinct in reputation repair is to address the accusation directly — to produce content that says "here's what we actually do" and trust the audience to update their priors. That instinct is almost always wrong. People don't change their minds about brands because brands tell them to. They change their minds when they see something that makes their existing belief feel like it doesn't apply to the company they're watching.

The creative strategy here is not to fight the surveillance narrative — it's to make the surveillance narrative feel like it belongs to a completely different kind of company than the one you just watched spend 90 seconds on a nurse and a flood and a 4am phone call.

Campaign platform
The insight that drives every execution

The campaign platform is built on a single reframe: Palantir doesn't collect data — it helps the institutions that already hold your data use it more humanely. Every execution puts a real person at the centre of a real decision. No dashboards. No servers. No abstract data visualisations. Just a nurse, a logistics manager, a disaster relief coordinator — and the moment where better information produced a better outcome for another human being.

"Your data, working for you."
Hero film — 90 sec
It was already there
A hospital already had the data to prevent a medication error. A city already had the data to reroute emergency services faster. The information existed. Palantir helped the people responsible for your care actually use it.
Campaign spot — 60 sec
The 4am call
A hospital administrator receives a critical resource alert at 4am. The spot shows what that call means for the patients on the floor by morning. No software. No data. Just the human consequence of an institution that could see what was coming.
Campaign spot — 60 sec
Same data. Different decision.
Two versions of the same flood relief scenario — identical data, two teams. One sees the pattern. One doesn't. The difference is not the information. It's the ability to act on it in time.

Scene breakdown — "The 4am call"
Shot-by-shot treatment for the 60-second lead execution

The following breakdown illustrates the execution at production level — demonstrating that the strategic concept has been thought through to the frame, not merely articulated as a premise.

0:00–0:06
A hospital corridor. 4:07am. Timestamp visible on a wall clock. The corridor is empty except for a single nurse at the end of the frame, back to camera, moving away. No dialogue. Ambient sound only.
0:06–0:14
A phone lights up on a desk. Close on the screen — a resource alert. The administrator's face in the background, out of focus, turns toward it. She picks it up. We see her reading, not the content. Her expression shifts — not alarm. Recognition.
0:14–0:28
A series of quiet, unhurried actions. A phone call made. A whiteboard updated. A colleague notified by text. None dramatised — shown at the pace at which they actually happen. The score enters: a single sustained cello note.
0:28–0:48
Six hours later. The same corridor, now busy. The morning shift. A patient being moved. Two nurses in conversation. The administrator walking the opposite direction — not looking at her phone now. The visual argument: the 4am call was the reason this morning is ordinary.
0:48–0:54
Cut to black. White text, no animation: "The data was already there." Two seconds of silence. The weight of the statement settles.
0:54–1:00
Palantir wordmark. White on black. No strapline. No product shot. No URL. The name is the final word — and by this point, it has earned the right to be.

Creative Strategy · Editorial Direction · Post-Production

Amir Razak is a creative strategist and editorial director whose practice is built at the intersection of analytical rigour and post-production craft — translating complex brand and audience intelligence into visual narratives that are both strategically precise and cinematically considered.

His path to creative strategy is unconventional by design. A rigorous quantitative background at NYU formed the analytical foundation; years of independent post-production practice — across documentary film, brand campaigns, literary publishing, and commercial advertising — built the editorial one. The result is a strategist who can read a data pipeline and a timeline with equal fluency.

Available for creative strategy, editorial direction, and performance creative roles across brand, agency, and in-house environments — with a demonstrated ability to drive measurable outcomes at every stage of the creative lifecycle.

Editorial philosophy
"Editing is not assembly. It is authorship — and the moment that distinction is surrendered to automation, the work loses the one thing no algorithm can manufacture: a point of view."

Razak approaches post-production as a discipline of consequence, not convenience. Every cut is a decision — about energy, about emotional register, about what the audience is permitted to feel and when. That decision-making capacity is the product of thousands of hours of practice, a developed sensitivity to the relationship between audio and image, and an editorial voice built independently rather than inherited from a curriculum or a preset library.

He holds a considered position on the role of artificial intelligence in the creative process: AI is a legitimate research, ideation, and logistical tool, but its application as a substitute for editorial judgement produces work that is technically functional and creatively inert. The grain match, the motivated camera move, the precise moment a cut lands against a line of audio — these are not problems that benefit from computational optimisation. They are the work.

This philosophy is not a rejection of technology — it is a defence of authorship. The brands and projects that benefit most from Razak's practice are those that understand the difference.
